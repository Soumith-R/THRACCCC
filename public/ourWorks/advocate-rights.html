Advocate Rights

Rights of Advocates in India

Advocates in India are governed by the Advocates Act, 1961 and enjoy certain legal rights that enable them to perform their professional duties effectively while upholding justice. These rights are also in line with the principles of the Indian Constitution, ensuring that advocates can work freely, independently, and without fear.

Key Rights of Advocates

  1. Right to Practice (Section 30, Advocates Act, 1961)
    • Every advocate whose name is entered in the State roll is entitled to practice in all courts, before any tribunal, or any person legally authorized to take evidence throughout India.
    • This right is subject to the rules framed by the Bar Council of India (BCI).
  2. Right to Freedom of Speech and Expression (Article 19(1)(a) of the Constitution)
    • Advocates have the right to freely argue cases and express legal opinions in courts, subject to professional ethics and court decorum.
  3. Right to Enter Any Court
    • An advocate has the right to enter any court in India, including the Supreme Court and High Courts, for the purpose of representing a client, subject to court rules.
  4. Right to Meet Clients Freely
    • Advocates have the right to meet and consult their clients in prison or any place where they are confined, to ensure proper legal representation.
  5. Right to Refuse a Case
    • An advocate has the right to refuse to accept a case if it conflicts with their principles, professional ethics, or if there is a reasonable cause.
  6. Right to Protection Under Law
    • Under various state-specific Advocates Protection Acts and provisions of criminal law, an advocate is protected from violence, intimidation, or obstruction while performing their duties.
  7. Right Right to Fees
    • Advocates are entitled to charge reasonable professional fees for the services rendered, as agreed with the client.
  8. Right to be Enrolled with a Bar Council
    • To practice in any court, an advocate must be enrolled with a State Bar Council and be eligible to appear for the All India Bar Examination (AIBE) conducted by the Bar Council of India.

Constitutional Link

The rights of advocates are rooted in the constitutional principles of justice (Preamble), right to equality (Article 14), and freedom of profession (Article 19(1)(g)), ensuring that the legal profession remains independent, dignified, and essential to the functioning of a democratic society.
